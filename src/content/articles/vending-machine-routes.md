A vending machine route is a set of placed, income-producing machines sold as a package: the equipment, the locations, and the service schedule. Routes typically sell for 12 to 24 months of net profit, so a route netting $2,000 monthly commonly lists between $24,000 and $48,000, with equipment quality and location contracts moving the price inside that band.

Buying a route is the shortcut past the slowest part of this business, which is landing locations one pitch at a time. It is also where beginners overpay most, because sellers quote gross revenue and buyers forget to verify anything. This guide covers how routes are priced, what the multiple actually depends on, and the due diligence list we would run before wiring money.

What you are actually buying

A route purchase bundles three assets of very different quality. The machines are the tangible part: count them, age them, and price them against the used market (our pricing bands are at VendingStartup/used-snack-vending-machine-for-sale). The locations are the real value, and they are fragile; most location agreements let the host cancel with 30 days notice. The revenue history is the part you must verify, because it is the part most often inflated.

Notice what is usually not included: any guarantee the locations stay. A route where the top two locations produce 60 percent of revenue is much riskier than the same total spread across ten hosts. Concentration should lower the price you offer.

How routes are priced

*Example asking prices for a route netting $2,000/month at common multiples. Illustrative figures.*

The working convention in route sales is a multiple of monthly or annual net profit. Operators and brokers commonly quote 12 to 24 months of net (1x to 2x annual net is the same math stated differently). Where a specific route lands depends on a short list of factors:

|                     |                              |                                          |
| ------------------- | ---------------------------- | ---------------------------------------- |
| **Factor**          | **Pushes price down**        | **Pushes price up**                      |
| Machine age         | 10+ years, no card readers   | Under 7 years, cashless installed        |
| Location agreements | Handshake deals, 30-day outs | Written multi-year agreements            |
| Revenue proof       | Seller's spreadsheet only    | Card-reader telematics + tax returns     |
| Concentration       | 2 locations = most of net    | Spread across 10+ hosts                  |
| Route density       | Scattered across 60 miles    | Tight 15-mile service loop               |
| Seller support      | Keys and a wave              | 30-60 day transition, host introductions |

A sanity check we always run: price the machines alone on the used market. If a $40,000 route contains $12,000 of equipment, you are paying $28,000 for locations and cash flow, and the revenue proof has to be strong enough to justify exactly that.

The due diligence checklist

  - Ride the route: service every machine with the seller at least once, ideally twice, before closing.

  - Verify revenue independently: card-reader dashboards (Nayax, Cantaloupe) are hard to fake; cash-heavy claims deserve a discount.

  - Ask for 12+ months of records and, on larger deals, tax returns. Sellers who refuse are answering your question.

  - Call or meet every location host: confirm the agreement, the commission, and that they intend to keep the machines.

  - Test the equipment like a used-machine buyer: motors, validators, compressors, MDB ports.

  - Get location agreements assigned to you in writing, not assumed (contract terms are covered at VendingStartup/vending-machine-contract).

  - Check for exclusivity conflicts and confirm licensing transfers; vending licensing varies by state and locality.

  - Put a non-compete and a transition period in the purchase agreement, and have a lawyer review it.

A worked example

Say a listed route claims $3,400 monthly gross across nine machines. Card telematics confirm $3,100. Product costs run 45 percent, commissions average 8 percent, and card and service costs take another $120, leaving verified net around $1,460 per month. At 15 to 20 months of net, a defensible offer sits between $22,000 and $29,000. If the seller is asking $45,000 on the strength of the unverified $3,400 figure, that gap is the negotiation, and walking away is a fine outcome. Financing note: routes sometimes qualify for small business loans, and the SBA explains funding options for buying an existing business at https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/plan-your-business/fund-your-business. Most small route deals, though, close with cash or seller financing over 12 to 24 months.

Build versus buy

Building a route yourself costs less cash and more time: roughly $2,000 to $3,000 per placed machine and a lot of location pitching (the full sequence is at VendingStartup/starting-a-vending-machine-business). Buying compresses two years of grinding into one transaction at a premium. Many operators do both, starting with two or three self-placed machines to learn the work, then buying a route once they can judge one. Structuring the deal: payment, transition, and protection

Once the price is agreed, the structure decides how much risk you actually carry. Three mechanisms do most of the protective work in small route deals.

Seller financing with a holdback. Paying 50 to 70 percent at closing and the remainder over 6 to 18 months keeps the seller invested in a clean handoff, and it converts revenue claims into something the seller has to stand behind. If locations walk in month two, a negotiated offset against the remaining payments is your remedy. Sellers who insist on 100 percent cash at closing for an unverified route are telling you where the risk sits.

A real transition period. Thirty to sixty days of the seller riding along, introducing you to every host, and handing over service notes is worth more than a price concession. Location hosts stay for relationships and service quality; a warm handoff preserves both, and a route bought without one commonly loses a location or two purely to the surprise of a stranger showing up with keys.

A written non-compete. Scope it reasonably (the service area, two to three years) so it is enforceable, and have a lawyer draft or review it along with the purchase agreement. The seller knows every host personally and could rebuild the route around you in a season; this clause is the only thing that prevents it. None of this is legal advice, and a few hundred dollars of attorney review on a $30,000 purchase is the cheapest insurance in the deal.

Red flags in route listings

  - Revenue quoted as gross with no cost breakdown; the multiple only means anything applied to net.

  - "Huge potential" doing the work that trailing twelve-month numbers should do.

  - Machines the seller cannot open in front of you, or telematics access they will not share.

  - Locations the seller will not let you contact before closing ("I do not want to spook them").

  - A route assembled recently from placement-company locations; those placements often churn fast.

  - Price far below market for claimed revenue. Underpriced routes with real numbers sell to the seller's operator friends before they ever hit a listing site.

One or two of these can have innocent explanations. Three is a pattern, and the correct response to a pattern is a lower offer contingent on verification, or a pass. Routes come to market constantly; the discipline to wait for a verifiable one is the highest-return skill in route buying.

After the purchase: the first 60 days

Treat the first two service cycles as an audit with a smile. Service every machine yourself, on the seller's schedule, and log actuals against the numbers you bought. Introduce yourself to every host early, confirm the commission terms in writing where anything was verbal, and fix every small mechanical complaint fast; new ownership is when hosts decide whether the change was good news. Expect one location to wobble regardless of how well you bought. Budgeting for the loss of the weakest host, in both the price you paid and your first-year plan, is what separates a route purchase that felt fine from one that felt like a rescue.

FAQ

How much do vending machine routes cost?

Routes commonly sell for 12 to 24 months of verified net profit. Small starter routes run $10,000 to $30,000, and established routes netting $3,000 to $5,000 monthly often list between $50,000 and $120,000. Equipment quality and written location agreements move prices within those bands.

Are vending machine routes for sale a good investment?

A verified route at 15 to 20 months of net can pay back in under two years, which is strong. The risk is location loss: most hosts can cancel on 30 days notice, so a route with revenue concentrated in one or two locations deserves a much lower multiple.

How do I verify a route's revenue?

Trust telematics over spreadsheets. Card-reader dashboards from providers like Nayax or Cantaloupe show actual sales by machine. Ride the route during service visits, count the cash pulls yourself, and ask for tax returns on any deal above roughly $30,000.

Can I finance a vending route purchase?

Sometimes. Seller financing over 12 to 24 months is the most common structure for small routes. Larger acquisitions may qualify for small business loans; SBA.gov outlines funding options for buying an existing business. Have a lawyer review any purchase agreement before signing.
