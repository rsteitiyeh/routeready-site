To find a location for a vending machine, target sites with 100 or more people present daily, few nearby food options, and dwell time (people waiting or working, not passing through). Then pitch the decision maker in person with a one-page offer. Expect 15 to 30 pitches per signed location; that ratio is normal.

Location is the single biggest driver of vending profit, bigger than machine type, product mix, or pricing. The same machine can gross $75 or $600 a month depending on where it sits. This guide gives you the foot-traffic math for judging a site before you commit, the target list we actually use, and the pitch that gets a yes.

The foot-traffic math

Here is the estimation formula we use, and the one behind the VendingStartup/calculator's location screen: daily people on site, times a buy rate, times days active, times average vend price. Operators commonly report buy rates around 2 to 5 percent of daily traffic for snack machines in workplaces, higher where people are captive (no cafeteria, far from stores) and lower in pass-through spaces.

(Table assumes 22 active days and $1.75 average vend.) The honest caveat: buy rate is the soft number in the formula. A 24/7 warehouse with no food nearby can beat 5 percent; an office one floor above a food court will not hit 2 percent. Captivity, meaning how hard it is to get food elsewhere, moves the number more than raw headcount does.

The best location types for a vending machine

  - Offices and call centers with 40+ employees and no cafeteria: the classic starter target.

  - Warehouses, distribution centers, and manufacturing floors: shift workers, high captivity, 24/7 demand.

  - Auto shops and dealerships: customers wait 30 to 90 minutes with nothing to do.

  - Apartment complexes (100+ units) near laundry rooms: steady evening traffic.

  - Laundromats: 45-minute forced dwell time; strong for snacks and candy.

  - Gyms and rec centers: drink-heavy demand; expect 10 to 15 percent commission asks.

  - Schools, hospitals, and government sites: high volume but formal bidding and nutrition rules; not beginner territory.

Scout by driving your own 10 to 15 minute radius first. Route density is worth more than a slightly better location across town, since your profit per service hour depends on drive time (VendingStartup/vending-machine-profit shows how that flows through the P\&L).

How to pitch a location owner

Find the actual decision maker: the office manager, owner, or facilities lead, not the front desk. Go in person, mid-morning on a weekday, and keep it under two minutes: you run a local vending business, you will place and service a machine at no cost to them, keep it stocked and clean, handle every repair, and they get a break-room amenity their staff will ask about exactly never. Offer commission only if they raise it; many small locations expect nothing, and typical asks run 10 to 25 percent at larger sites (the negotiation ranges are in our contract walkthrough at VendingStartup/vending-machine-contract).

Leave a one-page flyer with your phone number and follow up once by phone after three or four days. Track every pitch in a spreadsheet. Fifteen to thirty pitches per yes is the commonly reported ratio for cold outreach, and it improves fast once you have referenceable locations. When a host says yes, sign a simple location agreement the same week; handshake placements evaporate.

Judging a site before you commit a machine

Before moving 700 pounds of equipment, verify the numbers you were quoted. Visit at two different times of day and count people yourself. Check the competition radius: a vending bank in the next suite or a convenience store across the parking lot caps your buy rate. Confirm the practical details: a standard outlet, ground-floor access or an elevator, and a spot visible from the main traffic path. Corner placements behind doors quietly kill sales.

Then run the specific site through the VendingStartup/calculator with a conservative buy rate. If it does not clear your payback bar at 2 percent, pass or place a candy head instead (VendingStartup/candy-vending-machines-for-sale covers that lower-stakes option). One more practicality: some cities require a per-machine license before placement, and vending licensing varies by state and locality; the SBA's permit overview at https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/apply-licenses-permits plus one call to the city clerk settles it.

Your first location, then your route

Land one location, service it impeccably for a month, then ask the host for a referral; warm introductions convert far better than cold pitches. From there the game is density and repetition, machine by machine, and the whole sequence from registration to machine two is mapped in our playbook at VendingStartup/starting-a-vending-machine-business. The location scoring sheet we use

After enough site visits, we turned our gut checks into a scoring sheet so that decisions stopped depending on mood. Score each candidate location one to five on six factors, and the total tells you what to do.

|                 |                                       |                               |
| --------------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| **Factor**      | **What a 5 looks like**               | **What a 1 looks like**       |
| Daily people    | 300+ on site daily                    | Under 50                      |
| Captivity       | No food within a 10-minute round trip | Food court in the building    |
| Dwell time      | Shift workers, waiting rooms          | Pure pass-through corridor    |
| Hours of demand | 24/7 or multi-shift                   | Weekday 9-to-5 only           |
| Access and spot | Ground floor, visible from main path  | Basement corner behind a door |
| Host quality    | Engaged manager, written agreement    | Reluctant yes, handshake only |

Our thresholds: 24 or higher, place an electronic machine with confidence. Eighteen to 23, place with a conservative buy rate in your model and revisit at 90 days. Below 18, offer a candy head or walk away. The sheet takes five minutes per site and it has talked us out of placements that our optimism had already approved, which is exactly its job.

Keeping the location once you have it

Winning the spot is half the work; the other half is never giving the host a reason to reopen the decision. The retention basics are unglamorous and effective. Keep the machine full and clean, because the host sees it every day and their staff complains to them, not to you. Pay commission on time with a simple one-line sales report, even when the number is small, since nothing erodes trust like silence about money. Fix reported problems inside 48 to 72 hours and text the host when it is done. And check in personally every couple of months; a two-minute conversation surfaces the new facilities manager or the upcoming renovation before it becomes a 30-day termination letter.

Hosts who feel looked after also become your sales force. Office managers know other office managers, and a referred placement skips most of the 15-to-30-pitch grind. Operators commonly report that after the first five or six well-served locations, referrals and inbound requests supply a meaningful share of new placements. The route stops being cold-calling and starts being reputation, which is the point at which this business gets genuinely pleasant to run.

FAQ

What is the best location for a vending machine?

High-captivity workplaces: offices of 40+ people without a cafeteria, warehouses with shift workers, and waiting-time businesses like auto shops and laundromats. The common thread is 100+ people daily with dwell time and few food alternatives nearby.

How do I convince a business to host my vending machine?

Pitch the decision maker in person with a no-cost offer: you supply, stock, clean, and repair the machine, and their staff or customers get an amenity. Offer commission only if asked. Expect 15 to 30 pitches per yes on cold outreach, and sign a simple written agreement when they accept.

How much foot traffic does a vending machine need?

Around 100 people per day on site is the practical floor for an electronic snack machine. At a commonly reported 2 to 5 percent buy rate and a $1.75 average vend, 100 daily people produces roughly $75 to $195 in monthly gross; below that, place a bulk candy head instead.

Do vending machine placement companies work?

Some are legitimate brokers; many charge $300 to $1,500 per placement for locations that fail our traffic math. If you use one, refuse to pay until you have independently visited the site, counted traffic, and confirmed the host actually agreed. Doing your own pitching costs time instead of money and builds a referral network.
