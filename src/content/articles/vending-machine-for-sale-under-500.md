Under $500, you are buying mechanical bulk candy machines, not electronic snack or soda machines. A new single-head gumball machine runs $100 to $200, a triple-head candy machine $250 to $450, and used heads often sell for $30 to $80 each. Electronic machines at this price are almost always broken or missing parts.

That answer disappoints some readers, and we would rather disappoint you now than after a purchase. This article covers what each price tier under $500 really buys, the honest earnings math on bulk candy, and when a cheap machine is a smart first step versus a distraction.

The trap in this bracket is the as-is electronic machine. A $400 snack machine with a dead board and no card reader is not a $400 machine; after a control board, a bill validator, and moving costs, you have spent $1,000+ on 20-year-old equipment. If your budget can stretch to $1,500, a tested used machine is a different business entirely (we cover that market at VendingStartup/used-snack-vending-machine-for-sale).

What a bulk candy machine actually earns

Operators commonly report $10 to $40 per month gross per bulk candy head at decent locations, with standout spots doing more. Margins are excellent: a gumball costs about two cents and vends for a quarter, so 65 to 85 percent gross margin is normal. The problem is never margin. It is volume. Forty quarters is $10, and many locations do not produce forty impulse quarters a month.

Run the math on a triple-head machine: three heads at $15 to $25 each is $45 to $75 monthly gross, roughly $35 to $65 net after product, since there are no card fees or electricity. Many hosts take no commission on candy machines, though charity-label programs typically ask $1 to $2 per machine per month. A $350 machine at $50 net pays back in about seven months, which is genuinely good payback. It is just small dollars. When a cheap machine is the right move

A sub-$500 start makes sense in three situations. You want to learn location pitching and servicing with almost no downside. You are a minor or side-hustler who cannot yet move 700-pound equipment. Or you plan to run 20+ candy heads as a deliberate volume play, which some operators do profitably across restaurants and waiting rooms.

It is the wrong move if your actual goal is meaningful monthly income from one or two machines. In that case, save toward the $1,500 to $3,000 used electronic tier. Placement skills transfer; the revenue ceiling does not. Whichever path you take, the location process is the same and we walk through it at VendingStartup/find-location-for-vending-machine.

Buying checklist for cheap machines

  - Spin every head with a coin: the mechanism should turn smoothly and dispense a consistent portion.

  - Check the coin mechanism for rust or jams; replacements cost $15-$30 but sourcing them wastes weeks.

  - Confirm keys are included for both the coin box and the globe or hopper.

  - For metal stands, check the base plate; a wobbly candy machine gets tipped and emptied.

  - New machines: buy from vending suppliers, not general marketplaces, for parts support.

One admin note that surprises people: even a single gumball machine is a business in most jurisdictions. Vending licensing varies by state and locality, and many states apply sales tax to vended candy. The basics of registering and licensing a small business are laid out at https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/apply-licenses-permits, and your city clerk can tell you if a per-machine license applies.

A 90-day plan for a candy machine start

Cheap equipment does not mean casual execution, and the operators who get value out of this bracket run it like a real business at miniature scale. Here is the sequence we suggest.

Weeks one and two: order a new triple-head machine ($250 to $450) and register the basics. A sales tax permit and a check with the city clerk on per-machine licensing covers most jurisdictions, and it typically costs less than the candy. While you wait for delivery, build a target list of 20 locations within ten minutes of home: restaurants with waiting areas, barbershops, laundromats, auto shops, anywhere people stand around with change in their pockets.

Weeks three and four: pitch in person. The candy pitch is easier than the snack-machine version because you are asking for two square feet and no outlet. Offer either a charity-label arrangement or a simple 10 percent of gross; most small hosts take the charity option or nothing at all. Expect five to ten pitches per yes at this tier, better than the 15 to 30 typical for electronic machines.

Weeks five through twelve: service monthly, weigh the product you add (a kitchen scale turns refills into data), and record gross per head per month. At day 90 you will know which of the three heads earns and which is dead weight, and whether the location clears $15 per head. Below that, move the machine; candy machines relocate in one car trip, which is exactly why this tier is such cheap tuition.

The hidden costs even cheap machines have

Product waste is the main one. Chocolate blooms in heat, gum goes stale in slow globes, and a $30 globe of candy that turns over twice a year loses money to staleness before it loses to anything else. Match the globe size to the location's pace or run globes half full. Beyond that, budget for a lock replacement or two ($20 to $30 each), occasional mech springs, and the gas for a monthly service loop. None of it is large. All of it comes out of margins that are measured in quarters, which is why per-head tracking matters even when the numbers feel too small to bother with.

Scaling past the first machine

If the first triple-head clears your floor for three straight months, the expansion decision is pleasantly simple: candy scales by copy-paste. Buy the identical machine, place it in the same ring, service both in one trip. Operators typically add heads in batches of three (one machine) and stop expanding the moment service day stops fitting in an afternoon, because at candy margins the route only works while the labor stays trivial. The alternative expansion path is vertical: keep the candy route as cash flow and put its profits toward a used electronic machine, which is how a lot of snack operators quietly got their start. Neither path is wrong. The mistake is expanding a candy route that has not yet proven its per-head floor, which just multiplies a mediocre result.

FAQ

Can I get a real snack vending machine for under $500?

Rarely, and almost never one worth owning. Electronic snack machines under $500 are typically sold as-is with dead boards, bad validators, or missing keys, and repairs push total cost past $1,000. Under $500, mechanical bulk candy machines are the realistic purchase.

How much does a gumball machine make per month?

Operators commonly report $10 to $40 gross per head per month at decent locations. Margins run 65 to 85 percent since gumballs cost about two cents each, so a triple-head machine often nets $35 to $65 monthly with no electricity or card fees.

Where can I buy a cheap vending machine?

Used heads show up on Facebook Marketplace and Craigslist for $30 to $250. New bulk candy machines from vending supply companies run $100 to $450 and come with parts support, which matters more than the price difference over a few years of service.

Do cheap candy machines need a license?

Often yes. Vending machine licensing varies by state and locality; many states require a sales tax permit for vended candy and some cities license each machine. Check your city clerk and state revenue department, and see SBA.gov for general licensing guidance.
