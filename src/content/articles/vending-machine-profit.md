A single well-placed snack or drink vending machine commonly nets $100 to $300 per month in profit after product costs, location commission, and card fees. Gross revenue per machine ranges widely, from under $75 monthly at weak locations to $600 or more at strong ones, so the location drives the profit far more than the machine does.

That is the answer the hype sellers will not give you. Search this topic and you will find courses promising thousands per machine. Operators who actually run routes report a much wider and much lower distribution, and we would rather you build a plan on the boring numbers. Below is the full profit math: revenue ranges, margins by product type, a worked monthly P\&L, and where the model actually scales.

Average vending machine profit: the honest ranges

There is no reliable national average, and anyone quoting one to the dollar is inventing it. What we can say from operator reports and route sale listings is this: most snack/soda placements gross $200 to $400 per month, weak ones do under $100, and the best office and industrial placements do $500 to $800 or more. Net profit typically lands at 40 to 55 percent of gross for snack and drink machines after all variable costs.

|                                |                             |                           |
| ------------------------------ | --------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| **Location quality**           | **Monthly gross (typical)** | **Monthly net (typical)** |
| Weak (low traffic, poor fit)   | under $100                  | under $50                 |
| Average starter placement      | $200-$400                   | $100-$200                 |
| Good office or shop floor      | $400-$600                   | $200-$330                 |
| Strong industrial/high-traffic | $600-$1,000+                | $300-$550+                |

Treat that table as a planning tool, not a promise. Two identical machines 10 minutes apart can differ by 5x on revenue. That is why we tell people to obsess over placement, and why our walkthrough on how to find a location for a vending machine (VendingStartup/find-location-for-vending-machine) is the most useful page on this site.

Bulk candy is the margin king at 65 to 85 percent (a $0.02 gumball sells for $0.25), but the absolute dollars per machine are tiny. Snacks and drinks run 40 to 60 percent with far higher volume. Vending machine profit per month, at scale

One machine at $134 net is gas money. The model works because servicing machine number five barely takes more of your week than machine number one if the route is geographically tight. Ten average machines at $150 average net is $1,500 per month for roughly 15 to 20 hours of monthly work. Thirty machines is a job replacement for many operators, and at that point most people are buying existing routes rather than placing one machine at a time. Our route valuation guide (VendingStartup/vending-machine-routes) covers what those cost.

The failure mode is also worth naming. Machines in dead locations still consume inventory (expired product), time, and attention. A route of ten machines where four are duds can net less than five good machines. Prune ruthlessly.

What moves the profit number most

  - Foot traffic and captivity: 100+ people daily with few nearby alternatives is the baseline for good numbers.

  - Cashless payment: operators commonly report 15 to 35 percent sales lifts after adding a card reader.

  - Commission: every 10 points of commission takes roughly 20 to 25 percent of your net at typical margins.

  - Product mix: dead slots earn zero; per-slot tracking and quick swaps are the cheapest revenue lever you have.

  - Route density: profit per hour, not per machine, is the real metric once you pass three machines.

One structural note: your legal and tax setup affects take-home too. Business structure basics are laid out plainly at https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure, and vending licensing varies by state and locality, so confirm local requirements before projecting net income.

Cash flow timing: the part the margin math hides

Profit percentages describe a month; cash flow describes your bank account, and the two diverge early on. You buy inventory before it sells, so every new machine ties up $300 to $600 in stock that returns to you over the following weeks. Card revenue settles on a delay of a few days to a month depending on the processor, while cash is yours the moment you pull it. And repairs arrive in lumps: months of smooth $150 nets, then a $350 compressor call that erases two of them.

The practical rule we give new operators: keep a float of one full restock plus one typical repair (call it $600 to $1,000 per two or three machines) before counting any of the net as spendable. Operators who skip the float end up under-stocking machines to conserve cash, which cuts sales, which tightens cash further. It is the one downward spiral in this business that is entirely self-inflicted and entirely avoidable.

How profit compounds when you reinvest

The quiet strength of vending is that the profits are large relative to the cost of the next machine. A three-machine route netting $450 a month generates enough in five to six months to buy and place machine four. Run that loop patiently and the route grows without outside money: operators commonly go from one machine to eight or ten in two to three years on reinvested profits alone.

That table is an example trajectory, not a forecast; it assumes average placements and no disasters, and real routes grow in lurches rather than lines. But the mechanism is real. Each machine is a small cash engine that funds the next one, and the operators who compound fastest are the ones who prune weak locations quickly instead of nursing them. A machine that nets $40 a month is not a small win. It is capital parked in the wrong spot.

The other compounding lever is time efficiency. Your tenth machine takes the same service visit as your first, but by then you know exactly what sells, your route is geographically tight, and your per-visit time drops from 40 minutes to 20. Net profit per hour, the metric that actually matters, roughly doubles between machine one and machine ten for most operators, independent of any change in per-machine revenue.

Benchmarks: how to know if your machine is underperforming

New operators often cannot tell whether a slow machine is normal or broken, so here are the checkpoints we use. By the end of month one at a workplace location, the machine should be doing at least four vends a day; below that, something is wrong with the placement, the products, or the prices, in roughly that order of likelihood. By month three, a healthy placement settles into a stable weekly gross that moves less than 20 percent week to week outside holidays. And by month six you should know your per-visit numbers cold: gross since last visit, units sold, cash versus card split, and spoilage pulled.

When a machine misses those marks, work the checklist in order. Confirm the machine is visible and working every day (hosts rarely report outages; they just stop noticing the machine). Compare your prices against the nearest alternative, not against other vending machines. Swap the three worst slots. Only after those three fixes fail should you conclude the location is the problem, and at that point move the machine promptly. Operators commonly report that relocated machines resolve within one cycle: either the new location proves the machine was fine, or the same weak numbers follow it and you have a machine problem after all. Both answers are worth the cost of a move.

The last benchmark is your own hourly rate. Total monthly net divided by total monthly hours, including buying trips and bookkeeping, should clear what your time is worth by month six. If it does not, the fix is almost never harder work. It is density, pruning, and better locations, in that order.

FAQ

How much profit does a vending machine make per month?

Most snack and drink machines net $100 to $300 per month after product costs, commission, and card fees. Weak placements net under $50 and strong industrial or office placements can net $300 to $550 or more. Location quality explains most of the spread.

What is the average vending machine profit margin?

Gross margins run about 40 to 60 percent on snacks and drinks and 65 to 85 percent on bulk candy. After commissions, card fees, and service costs, net margins on gross sales typically land between 30 and 50 percent for snack/soda machines.

Can you live off vending machines?

Yes, but it takes scale. At a typical $150 net per machine per month, replacing a $4,500 monthly income takes roughly 30 well-placed machines, which is a real route with weekly service work, not passive income. Many full-time operators got there by buying existing routes.

How many vends per day is good?

For a snack/soda machine, 6 to 10 vends per day is a solid placement and 15 or more is excellent. At a $1.75 average price, 8 daily vends is roughly $420 in monthly gross, which nets $130 to $180 at typical cost structures.
