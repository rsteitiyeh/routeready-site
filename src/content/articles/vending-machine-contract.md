A vending machine contract, usually called a location agreement, is a short written deal between the machine owner and the property owner covering commission (typically 10 to 25 percent of gross sales, and often 0 percent at small locations), placement, electricity, service access, term length, and how either side can exit. One to two pages handles most placements.

This article is educational, not legal advice; have a lawyer review any agreement before you sign or ask a host to sign. What we can give you is the operator's view: what these agreements normally contain, what commission structures look like in practice, and the clauses that prevent the disputes we have actually seen.

Commission structures in the real world

![](/media/fde330b00658ac1e95ea4d0b9486aa02128aa604.png)

*Commission ranges operators commonly report by location type. Approximate; everything is negotiable.*

Commission is a percentage of gross sales paid to the host, usually monthly or quarterly. Operators commonly report these bands: small offices and shops often charge nothing (the machine itself is the amenity), gyms and apartment buildings ask 5 to 15 percent, larger offices 10 to 20 percent, and high-traffic retail or institutional spots 15 to 25 percent. Above 25 percent the math stops working for most snack machines; at a typical cost structure, every 10 points of commission removes roughly a fifth of your net profit, a relationship you can test in the VendingStartup/calculator.

Two alternatives to percentage deals show up regularly. Flat monthly rent ($25 to $100) suits hosts who want predictability, and it favors you at high-volume spots. Product credit (free vends for staff) works at small offices and costs you pennies on the dollar. Never agree to a commission on gross when you can tie it to net collected, and read on for why.

The clauses that matter

|                         |                                             |                                                       |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------- |
| **Clause**              | **What it covers**                          | **Operator note**                                     |
| Parties and premises    | Who, and exactly where the machine sits     | Name the specific spot; relocations kill sales        |
| Term and renewal        | Length, often 1 year auto-renewing          | Longer terms raise your route's resale value          |
| Commission              | Rate, base (gross vs net), payment schedule | Define gross precisely; exclude refunds and sales tax |
| Termination             | Notice period, usually 30-60 days           | Symmetrical notice protects both sides                |
| Utilities               | Who pays electricity (host, normally)       | A snack machine draws little; combos draw more        |
| Access                  | Your right to service during business hours | 24/7 access matters for industrial sites              |
| Exclusivity             | You are the only vending provider on site   | The most valuable clause you can get                  |
| Liability and insurance | Damage, injury, machine ownership           | State the machine remains your property               |

The exclusivity clause deserves emphasis. A competitor placing a machine ten feet from yours can halve your revenue overnight, and without exclusivity you have no recourse. It costs nothing to ask and hosts rarely object.

A simple agreement, section by section

Here is the structure we use for a standard placement, as an educational walkthrough. Section 1 identifies both parties and the premises, down to "the second-floor break room." Section 2 grants you the right to place, own, operate, and remove the machine, stating it stays your personal property and never becomes a fixture. Section 3 sets the term: one year, auto-renewing, with 30 days written notice to end. Section 4 handles money: the commission rate, what counts as gross sales (collected sales net of refunds and sales tax), and payment by the 15th of the following month with a simple sales report.

Section 5 covers operations: host provides the outlet and the space, you handle stocking, maintenance, and repairs within a stated response time (48 to 72 hours is common). Section 6 is exclusivity. Section 7 covers liability: you carry general liability insurance, the host is not responsible for machine damage beyond negligence, and vandalism or theft is your risk. Section 8 is boilerplate: governing state, entire agreement, signatures. That is genuinely the whole document for most placements. Have a lawyer in your state review your template once; then you reuse it for every location.

Negotiating without losing the location

Most hosts care less about commission than about the machine being full, working, and not their problem. Lead the pitch with service (we cover the full pitch at VendingStartup/find-location-for-vending-machine), offer a fair rate for the location type, and trade points for term: an extra 2 percent commission is worth it for a two-year term with exclusivity, since written multi-year agreements directly raise what your route sells for later (VendingStartup/vending-machine-routes explains that valuation math).

Keep a signed copy, a countersigned copy for the host, and a scan in your records. If you form an LLC, sign as the LLC. Business structure basics are at https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure, and note that vending licensing varies by state and locality, so your agreement should not be your only paperwork.

Mistakes operators make with location agreements

The most common mistake is not having one. Handshake placements feel friendly right up until the host's cousin starts a vending business, and without a written term and notice period you have no standing at all. The second most common is signing the host's document without reading the commission base: an agreement that pays commission on "gross sales" without excluding sales tax and card fees quietly pays the host on money you never kept. Define the base as collected sales net of refunds, sales tax, and processing fees, and the same percentage costs you meaningfully less.

Two subtler ones round out the list. Agreeing to vague service standards ("machine will be kept stocked") invites disputes; a concrete standard ("restocked at least every 14 days, repairs addressed within 72 hours") protects you by being checkable. And forgetting removal logistics: your agreement should state that on termination you have 15 to 30 days of access to remove the machine. Operators have genuinely lost machines to locations that changed locks after a dispute, and that clause is what gets them back without drama.

When the host wants changes

Expect pushback on three clauses and know your lines in advance. On commission, trade rather than cave: an extra two points is a fair price for a two-year term or an exclusivity clause, and a bad rate with no term is the worst of both. On termination notice, hold at 30 days minimum; anything shorter makes your placement disposable and your route less sellable. On liability, hosts sometimes ask you to cover any damage near the machine; keep your responsibility scoped to the machine itself and your own negligence, which is what your general liability policy actually covers.

Corporate locations and property management companies will often insist on their own vendor agreement, sometimes ten pages of it. Those are usually fine but not always; watch for indemnification clauses that reach beyond your operations and for insurance minimums above a small operator's policy. This is precisely the moment the "have a lawyer review it" advice stops being boilerplate. An hour of review costs less than one month of a good location's net, and you only need it once per template.

A note on renewals

Renewal time is leverage time, in both directions. Before an auto-renewal date passes, review the location's twelve-month numbers. Strong placements deserve a proactive conversation: confirm the host is happy, consider a small commission bump in exchange for extending the term, and lock in exclusivity if you never got it. Weak placements deserve honesty; renegotiating to a lower commission at a fading location is routine and hosts usually prefer it to losing the machine. Calendar every agreement's renewal date the day you sign it. The operators who get surprised by terminations are almost always the ones who let renewals happen silently for years.

FAQ

What commission should I pay a vending machine location?

Operators commonly report 0 percent at small offices, 5 to 15 percent at gyms and apartments, and 10 to 25 percent at larger or high-traffic locations. Tie the percentage to collected sales net of refunds and sales tax, and get exclusivity in exchange.

Do I need a written contract for a vending machine location?

Strongly yes, even at friendly small locations. A one-to-two page agreement covering commission, term, access, exclusivity, and machine ownership prevents the common disputes and makes your route far more valuable if you ever sell it. Have a lawyer review your template.

Who pays for the electricity a vending machine uses?

The host, in nearly all standard agreements. A modern snack machine draws relatively little power, and refrigerated combo machines draw more, so name the responsibility explicitly in the utilities clause rather than leaving it assumed.

Can a location kick my vending machine out?

Usually yes, with notice. Most agreements allow either party to terminate with 30 to 60 days written notice. That is why concentration risk matters when buying routes and why longer terms with auto-renewal are worth trading commission points to get.
